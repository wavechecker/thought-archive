## Intro

Hypertension — high blood pressure — affects more than one billion people worldwide and is one of the leading modifiable causes of heart attack, stroke, kidney failure, and premature death. Despite its prevalence and the gravity of its consequences, hypertension typically causes no symptoms. Most people are unaware they have it until a routine blood pressure check or, in some cases, a cardiovascular event.

This guide explains what blood pressure numbers mean, who is at risk, how hypertension is diagnosed and managed, and how to monitor blood pressure at home effectively.

---

## Key Points

- Hypertension is defined as sustained blood pressure of 130/80 mmHg or higher (many current guidelines) or 140/90 mmHg (UK/European guidelines).
- It is largely asymptomatic — the only way to know your blood pressure is to measure it.
- Over one billion people worldwide have hypertension; fewer than half have it adequately controlled.
- Untreated hypertension is a leading cause of heart attack, stroke, kidney failure, and dementia.
- Lifestyle changes reduce blood pressure meaningfully; most people with established hypertension also require medication.
- Home blood pressure monitoring improves diagnostic accuracy and supports long-term management.

## Background

Blood pressure is the force that blood exerts against artery walls as the heart pumps it around the body. It is expressed as two numbers in millimetres of mercury (mmHg):

- **Systolic pressure** (higher number): pressure in the arteries when the heart beats.
- **Diastolic pressure** (lower number): pressure when the heart rests between beats.

A reading around 120/80 mmHg is generally considered healthy for most adults. Blood pressure naturally varies throughout the day — rising with activity and stress, falling during sleep — which is why a single measurement is not sufficient for diagnosis.

| Category | Systolic (mmHg) | Diastolic (mmHg) |
|---|---|---|
| Normal | Below 120 | Below 80 |
| Elevated | 120–129 | Below 80 |
| Stage 1 hypertension | 130–139 | 80–89 |
| Stage 2 hypertension | 140 or higher | 90 or higher |
| Hypertensive crisis | Above 180 | Above 120 |

Thresholds vary slightly across guidelines. The 130/80 threshold is used by the American Heart Association/American College of Cardiology; UK and European guidelines use 140/90 mmHg as the primary diagnostic threshold. Your doctor will apply whichever is appropriate for your situation.

## Causes or Mechanisms

### Primary (essential) hypertension

Around 90–95% of hypertension cases are primary — meaning no single identifiable cause. It develops gradually through a combination of genetic predisposition and lifestyle factors:

- Excess sodium intake, which increases fluid retention and arterial stiffness
- Physical inactivity
- Overweight and obesity, particularly central adiposity
- Excessive alcohol consumption
- Chronic stress, which activates the sympathetic nervous system
- Ageing, which reduces arterial elasticity

Research also highlights that increased sympathetic (fight-or-flight) nervous system activity may play a larger role in sustaining elevated blood pressure than previously recognised, particularly in younger people with primary hypertension.

### Secondary hypertension

Around 5–10% of cases have an identifiable underlying cause:

- **Chronic kidney disease** — the most common cause of secondary hypertension
- **Renal artery stenosis** — narrowing of the arteries supplying the kidneys
- **Primary hyperaldosteronism** — excess aldosterone from the adrenal glands
- **Obstructive sleep apnoea** — repeated oxygen drops activate the renin-angiotensin system
- **Thyroid disorders** — both hypothyroidism and hyperthyroidism
- **Medications** — NSAIDs, oral contraceptives, decongestants, and some antidepressants

Secondary hypertension should be considered when blood pressure is difficult to control, when it occurs in younger people without obvious risk factors, or when other clinical clues are present.

## Diagnosis

### How blood pressure is measured

For accurate readings:
- Sit quietly for at least 5 minutes beforehand
- Sit with the back supported, arm resting at heart level
- Avoid caffeine, exercise, and smoking for 30 minutes before
- Use an appropriate cuff size — too small overestimates blood pressure
- Take at least two readings, 1 minute apart, and average them

### Office, ambulatory, and home monitoring

Because blood pressure varies, a single office reading is often insufficient for diagnosis. Options include:

- **24-hour ambulatory blood pressure monitoring (ABPM):** a portable device records blood pressure throughout the day and night; considered the gold standard.
- **Home blood pressure monitoring (HBPM):** practical, widely recommended; provides multiple readings in everyday conditions.

Diagnosis typically requires consistently elevated readings across multiple occasions or on ambulatory or home monitoring. Some people show elevated readings only in clinical settings — a phenomenon called **white-coat hypertension** — which does not carry the same cardiovascular risk as sustained hypertension.

## Treatment

Hypertension treatment combines lifestyle modification and, for most people, medication. The goal is to reduce blood pressure to target and reduce overall cardiovascular risk.

### Lifestyle modifications

Lifestyle changes can each reduce systolic blood pressure by 4–10 mmHg — comparable to a single antihypertensive medication:

- **Salt reduction:** limiting sodium to under 2,000 mg/day (approximately 5 g of salt) can reduce systolic blood pressure by 4–8 mmHg.
- **DASH diet:** rich in fruits, vegetables, whole grains, and low-fat dairy, limiting saturated fat; associated with reductions of 8–14 mmHg systolic.
- **Physical activity:** at least 150 minutes per week of moderate-intensity aerobic exercise reduces systolic blood pressure by 4–8 mmHg.
- **Weight reduction:** losing 1 kg of body weight reduces blood pressure by approximately 1 mmHg; even modest weight loss has meaningful impact.
- **Limiting alcohol:** reducing to no more than 1–2 standard drinks per day.
- **Stopping smoking:** does not directly lower blood pressure long-term but substantially reduces overall cardiovascular risk.

### Medications

Most people with hypertension require one or more medications. The main drug classes:

- **ACE inhibitors** (e.g., ramipril, lisinopril): relax blood vessels by blocking angiotensin II; protect the kidneys in those with diabetes or CKD. Common first-line choice.
- **ARBs** (e.g., losartan, candesartan): similar mechanism to ACE inhibitors; often used when ACE inhibitors cause a persistent dry cough.
- **Calcium channel blockers** (e.g., amlodipine): relax arterial smooth muscle. Particularly effective in older adults and people of African or Caribbean descent.
- **Thiazide diuretics** (e.g., indapamide, chlorthalidone): reduce fluid volume by increasing urinary sodium excretion.
- **Beta-blockers** (e.g., bisoprolol): reduce heart rate and cardiac output; used in specific situations including heart failure, post-heart attack, and angina.

Most people with moderate or severe hypertension require two or more medications in combination. Combination therapy is typically more effective with fewer side effects than a single drug at high dose. Treatment is individualized — many factors influence which medication is chosen.

## Home Blood Pressure Monitoring

Home monitoring is increasingly recommended to confirm a diagnosis, track the response to treatment, and detect white-coat or masked hypertension.

**What to use:** A validated upper-arm automatic monitor (wrist monitors are less reliable). Validated device lists are maintained by national hypertension societies such as the British and Irish Hypertension Society and the American Medical Association.

**Monitoring protocol:**
1. Sit quietly for 5 minutes before measuring
2. Sit with back supported, arm resting at heart level
3. Avoid caffeine, exercise, alcohol, and smoking for 30 minutes before
4. Take two readings, 1 minute apart, in the morning and evening
5. Do this for at least 4–7 consecutive days before a medical appointment
6. Record all readings and share them with your doctor

A home average of 135/85 mmHg or higher generally corresponds to hypertension (slightly lower than the office threshold, as home readings tend to be lower than office readings).

## When to Seek Urgent Care

**Hypertensive crisis** (blood pressure typically above 180/120 mmHg) with any of the following requires emergency care — call emergency services or go to an emergency department immediately:

- Severe headache
- Visual disturbances or sudden loss of vision
- Chest pain
- Shortness of breath
- Confusion or altered consciousness
- Signs of stroke: facial drooping, arm weakness, speech difficulty

If blood pressure is very high but there are no symptoms, contact your GP or a nurse promptly — this may be a hypertensive urgency that can be managed more gradually but should not be ignored.

## Risks, Benefits, and Prognosis

Effective blood pressure control substantially reduces cardiovascular risk:

- A 10 mmHg reduction in systolic blood pressure reduces stroke risk by approximately 35% and heart attack risk by approximately 20%.
- Treating hypertension in older adults significantly reduces dementia risk.
- Treatment reduces progression to kidney failure, heart failure, and atrial fibrillation.

Antihypertensive medications are generally well tolerated, but side effects vary by class and by individual. Switching medications or adjusting doses is common and is a normal part of finding the right regimen.

Long-term adherence is the main challenge. Many people feel entirely well and may underestimate the importance of continuing treatment. Stopping medication without medical advice typically causes blood pressure to return to previous levels and reinstates cardiovascular risk.

## FAQ

**Q: Does hypertension always cause symptoms?**
A: No. Hypertension is usually completely asymptomatic, even when severely elevated. Symptoms like headache, nosebleed, or dizziness are unreliable — they occur frequently in people with normal blood pressure and are absent in most people with high blood pressure. Regular measurement is the only reliable way to know your blood pressure.

**Q: Can stress cause hypertension?**
A: Acute stress raises blood pressure temporarily, but the evidence that chronic psychological stress alone causes sustained hypertension is less clear. Stress can contribute indirectly — through poor diet, physical inactivity, alcohol, and tobacco use — which do raise blood pressure. Stress management is part of a healthy lifestyle but is not a substitute for other treatments.

**Q: Is salt really that important?**
A: Yes, for many people. Sodium intake is one of the most consistent dietary determinants of blood pressure. Salt restriction has a measurable effect — particularly for those who are salt-sensitive, which is more common in older people and people of African or Caribbean descent. Salt reduction also enhances the effectiveness of antihypertensive medications.

**Q: Do I need to take blood pressure medication forever?**
A: For most people, yes. Hypertension is a chronic condition and blood pressure typically rises again when medication is stopped. However, if significant and sustained lifestyle changes are achieved — particularly major weight loss — your doctor may trial reducing or stopping medication under careful monitoring.

**Q: What blood pressure should I aim for on treatment?**
A: Target ranges are individualised. For most adults with hypertension, current guidelines suggest a target below 130/80 mmHg. In older adults or those with significant comorbidities, less stringent targets may be appropriate to avoid side effects such as dizziness. Your doctor will advise the right target for you.
