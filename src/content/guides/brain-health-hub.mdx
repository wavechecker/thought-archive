## Key Risk Factors for Brain Health

Many brain conditions share modifiable risk factors:

| Risk Factor | Why It Matters |
|---|---|
| High blood pressure | The single biggest driver of stroke and vascular dementia |
| Diabetes | Doubles stroke risk; accelerates cognitive decline |
| Smoking | Significantly raises stroke and dementia risk |
| Physical inactivity | Reduces cerebral blood flow and cognitive reserve |
| Poor sleep | Impairs amyloid clearance; linked to dementia development |

Managing these is the foundation of brain disease prevention.

---

## How to Protect Brain Health

Evidence points to five areas with the strongest impact:

- **Exercise** — 150 minutes of moderate aerobic activity weekly reduces stroke and dementia risk
- **Blood pressure control** — keeping systolic BP below 130 mmHg is one of the highest-yield interventions
- **Cognitive engagement** — learning, reading, and mentally stimulating activity builds cognitive reserve
- **Sleep** — 7–9 hours per night supports the brain's waste-clearance (glymphatic) system
- **Social connection** — isolation is an independent risk factor for dementia

## FAQ

**Q: What is the difference between a stroke and a TIA?**  
A: A stroke causes lasting brain damage due to a blocked or burst blood vessel. A TIA (transient ischaemic attack) produces the same symptoms but resolves within minutes to hours—with no permanent damage. A TIA is a serious warning sign: stroke risk is highest in the days immediately after.

**Q: Can dementia be prevented?**  
A: Not entirely, but up to 45% of dementia cases are linked to modifiable risk factors. The Lancet Commission identifies hypertension, physical inactivity, smoking, diabetes, poor sleep, and low cognitive engagement as key targets. Acting on these—particularly in midlife—meaningfully reduces risk.

**Q: What increases stroke risk?**  
A: The most significant modifiable risk factors are high blood pressure, smoking, atrial fibrillation, diabetes, high cholesterol, physical inactivity, and obesity. High blood pressure alone accounts for around half of all strokes.

**Q: Do brain training apps work?**  
A: Evidence is mixed. Apps like Lumosity improve performance on their own tasks but show limited transfer to real-world cognitive function. What does have good evidence: aerobic exercise, learning new complex skills (e.g. a language or instrument), and staying socially active.

**Q: When should I get brain imaging?**  
A: Imaging is not a routine screening tool. It is indicated after a stroke or TIA, for new unexplained neurological symptoms, or as part of a dementia diagnostic workup. Speak with your GP before pursuing private scans—incidental findings can cause unnecessary anxiety without clinical benefit.

**Q: What lifestyle changes matter most for brain health?**  
A: Managing blood pressure is the single highest-yield intervention. Beyond that: regular aerobic exercise, not smoking, treating sleep disorders, and maintaining social and cognitive engagement all have consistent evidence behind them.

**Q: Is stroke always sudden?**  
A: Yes—stroke symptoms appear abruptly. Sudden facial drooping, arm weakness, or speech difficulty are the hallmarks. If symptoms pass quickly, that may be a TIA—still a medical emergency requiring same-day assessment.
