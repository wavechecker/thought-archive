faq:
  - q: "What is the difference between an HPV test and a Pap smear?"
    a: "A Pap smear (cervical cytology) looks at cells from the cervix under a microscope to detect abnormal cell changes. An HPV test checks for the presence of high-risk strains of the human papillomavirus — the virus that causes nearly all cervical cancers. HPV testing detects the underlying risk before cells may have changed; Pap smears detect changes that have already occurred."
  - q: "Why have many countries switched to HPV-first screening?"
    a: "HPV testing is more sensitive than Pap smears for detecting high-grade cervical disease. A negative HPV test provides stronger reassurance, allowing longer intervals between screens. It also detects risk earlier in the process, before abnormal cells are present. For these reasons, HPV-primary screening has replaced Pap-smear-only programs in many countries."
  - q: "Does a positive HPV test mean I have cancer?"
    a: "No. A positive HPV test means a high-risk HPV infection has been detected. Most HPV infections clear on their own within 1–2 years without causing cancer. A positive result triggers further investigation (often cytology and/or colposcopy) to determine whether cell changes are present. Screening finds risk — it does not diagnose cancer."
  - q: "Does the HPV vaccine mean I no longer need cervical screening?"
    a: "No. Screening is still recommended for vaccinated people. The vaccine protects against the most common high-risk HPV types but not all strains. As vaccinated cohorts age through screening programs, guidelines may evolve, but current recommendations are to continue screening."
  - q: "How often should I have cervical screening?"
    a: "Intervals depend on your country's guidelines, your age, and your results. Many HPV-primary programs screen every 5 years with a negative HPV result, compared to 2–3 years previously with Pap smears. Follow your national program's recommendations and attend follow-up if invited."

## Intro

Cervical screening saves lives, but confusion around **Pap smears** and **HPV tests** causes many women to delay or avoid screening altogether.

They are related—but not the same.

---

## Key Differences at a Glance

- Pap smear → looks for **abnormal cervical cells**
- HPV test → looks for **high-risk HPV DNA**
- HPV testing finds risk **earlier**
- Pap smears detect **existing damage**

---

## Pap Smear (Cytology)

A Pap smear examines cervical cells under a microscope.

**Strength:**  
- Detects precancerous cell changes directly

**Limitations:**  
- Can miss early HPV infection  
- Lower sensitivity than HPV testing

---

## HPV Testing

HPV testing detects genetic material from cancer-causing HPV strains.

**Strength:**  
- Identifies risk before cell damage occurs  
- Higher sensitivity for cancer prevention

**Limitations:**  
- Does not show cell changes on its own

---

## Why Guidelines Are Changing

Because persistent HPV infection causes nearly all cervical cancers, detecting the virus earlier improves prevention. Many programs now use:

- Primary HPV testing  
- Pap smear only if HPV is detected
