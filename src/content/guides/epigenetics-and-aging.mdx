faq:
  - q: "What is epigenetics?"
    a: "Epigenetics refers to chemical modifications — such as DNA methylation and histone modifications — that regulate which genes are active, without changing the underlying DNA sequence. These patterns shift measurably with age."
  - q: "What do epigenetic clocks measure?"
    a: "Epigenetic clocks use patterns of DNA methylation to estimate biological age. They correlate with aging-related outcomes but are not direct measures of how fast each organ is aging."
  - q: "Can epigenetic age be reversed?"
    a: "Some interventions can shift clock readings in research settings, but whether this translates into meaningfully better long-term outcomes for individuals is still under active investigation."
  - q: "Are epigenetic aging tests available to consumers?"
    a: "Commercial epigenetic age tests exist, but their clinical utility is still limited. Results should be interpreted cautiously and not used to make major health decisions without medical guidance."
  - q: "Does lifestyle affect epigenetic aging?"
    a: "Yes. Sleep, diet, exercise, and chronic stress influence epigenetic patterns — one reason lifestyle is thought to modify biological aging trajectories, though the full clinical significance is still being studied."

## Intro

Epigenetics is often presented as the bridge between genes and environment. This guide will separate what is well-established from what is marketing.

## Key Points

- Epigenetics regulates gene expression without changing DNA sequence
- “Aging clocks” are biomarkers, not destiny
- Short-term clock changes don’t automatically mean long-term benefit

## FAQ

**Q: What is epigenetics?**  
A: Regulation of gene expression via chemical modifications (e.g., methylation) without changing DNA sequence.

**Q: What do epigenetic clocks measure?**  
A: DNA methylation patterns correlated with age and risk — informative, but not perfect.

**Q: Can you reverse epigenetic age?**  
A: Clock shifts are possible; outcome significance remains uncertain.
