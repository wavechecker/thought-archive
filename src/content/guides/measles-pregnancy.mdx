# Measles and Pregnancy

## Intro
Measles is highly contagious and can be **more serious in pregnancy**. Because **MMR is typically not given during pregnancy**, prevention relies on confirming immunity **before conception** and acting quickly if exposure happens.

For the full illness overview, see: **[Measles (Rubeola)](/guides/measles)**.

## Key Points
- If you might become pregnant, confirm **measles immunity** early  
- **MMR is usually avoided in pregnancy** (live attenuated vaccine)  
- If exposed while pregnant, seek **urgent clinical/public health advice**  
- Household/community vaccination helps protect you indirectly

## Why Measles Matters in Pregnancy
Measles can increase the risk of serious maternal illness and can also affect pregnancy outcomes. The key is not panic — it’s **prevention + speed**:
- Know your immunity status
- Avoid high-risk exposure settings during outbreaks
- Act fast after exposure

## Before Pregnancy: Best Protection
If you are planning pregnancy (or could become pregnant):
- Check your immunization record (look for **2 MMR doses**)  
- If uncertain, ask a clinician about testing and/or vaccination  
- If MMR is needed, it is usually given **before conception** with a recommended waiting interval afterward (follow local guidance)

<Aside title="Planning ahead matters" variant="info">
Because MMR is generally avoided during pregnancy, the best time to fix immunity gaps is **before pregnancy**.
</Aside>

## During Pregnancy: Reducing Exposure Risk
Practical risk reduction during outbreaks:
- Avoid crowded indoor settings where feasible  
- Ask close contacts to confirm vaccination if outbreaks are active  
- Consider masking in high-risk clinical or crowded environments (context-dependent)  
- Ensure household members are up to date with MMR unless contraindicated  

## What Counts as an Exposure?
Measles can remain in the air for up to **two hours** after an infectious person leaves. Possible exposures include:
- Household contact
- Waiting rooms / emergency departments
- Daycare/school settings
- Air travel or airports

If you are notified of exposure, treat it as time-sensitive.

## What To Do After Suspected Exposure
1) **Contact your clinician or local public health unit urgently**  
2) Tell them:
   - Pregnancy status and gestational age  
   - Vaccination records (or uncertainty)  
   - When/where exposure happened  
3) Follow local guidance on:
   - Whether immune globulin or other measures are recommended  
   - Monitoring period and symptom watch  
   - Isolation precautions if symptoms develop

<Aside title="Do not wait for symptoms" variant="warning">
Post-exposure decisions depend on timing. Call promptly even if you feel well.
</Aside>

## Symptoms to Watch For
Early symptoms can look like a cold or flu:
- Fever
- Cough
- Runny nose
- Red eyes  
Then:
- Koplik spots (mouth)
- Rash starting on the face and spreading

If symptoms begin, avoid public spaces and seek medical advice before attending clinics (to prevent spread).

## Breastfeeding and Newborn Considerations
Newborn protection depends heavily on maternal immunity and community coverage. If you’re postpartum and not immune, ask about the safest time to update vaccinations.

## FAQ
**Q: If I accidentally got MMR early in pregnancy, what happens?**  
A: Contact your clinician. Many guidelines treat this as a situation for counseling and documentation rather than automatic intervention, but care should be individualized.

**Q: Should my partner/household get vaccinated?**  
A: If they are not immune and have no contraindications, updating household vaccination helps protect you.

**Q: Can I travel while pregnant during an outbreak?**  
A: Sometimes, but risk depends on destination and immunity. See **[Measles and International Travel](/guides/measles-travel)**.
