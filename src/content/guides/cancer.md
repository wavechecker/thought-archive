# Cancer — Guide Hub

Cancer is not a single disease — it is a broad group of conditions in which abnormal cells grow out of control and can spread to other parts of the body. It is one of the leading causes of death globally, accounting for nearly 10 million deaths and almost 20 million new diagnoses each year. Yet many cancers are highly treatable, especially when detected early, and a substantial proportion of cases are preventable.

This hub connects our condition-specific cancer guides. Each disease hub covers that cancer in depth — what it is, how it is found and staged, the treatments available, and life beyond treatment. This parent hub also covers shared themes: what cancer is, how it develops, common warning signs, and the links between lifestyle and risk.

## Common Symptoms & When to Seek Urgent Help

**Symptoms that warrant prompt (non-emergency) medical review — do not ignore:**
- An unexplained or growing lump anywhere on the body
- Unexplained weight loss of more than 5% body weight
- Blood in stool, urine, sputum, or unusual vaginal bleeding
- Persistent cough, hoarseness, or difficulty swallowing
- Changes in a mole (size, shape, colour, bleeding)
- Persistent unexplained fatigue
- A sore that doesn't heal

**Seek emergency care immediately for:**
- Severe or uncontrolled bleeding
- Sudden shortness of breath or chest pain
- High fever with chills in someone on chemotherapy (possible neutropenic sepsis)
- Signs of spinal cord compression — sudden back pain with leg weakness or numbness
- Confusion, seizures, or sudden neurological change
- Signs of anaphylaxis after treatment

## FAQ

**Q: What is cancer?**
A group of diseases in which cells divide uncontrollably and may invade surrounding tissue or spread to other organs through the blood or lymphatic system.

**Q: What causes cancer?**
Genetic mutations — some inherited, most acquired through lifestyle and environmental exposures including tobacco, alcohol, UV radiation, certain infections, obesity, and physical inactivity.

**Q: What are the most common cancers?**
Globally: breast, lung, colorectal, prostate, and stomach. Lung cancer is the leading cause of cancer death.

**Q: What are early warning signs?**
An unexplained lump, unexplained weight loss, persistent fatigue, blood in stool or urine, a cough that won't go away, unusual bleeding, or a changing mole. Many early cancers cause no symptoms — screening catches them before symptoms develop.

**Q: Can cancer be prevented?**
Not completely, but up to 40–50% of cancers are linked to modifiable factors. Avoiding tobacco, limiting alcohol, maintaining healthy weight, sunscreen, HPV and hepatitis B vaccination, and attending screening programs all reduce risk significantly.

**Q: How is cancer diagnosed?**
Physical examination, imaging (CT, MRI, PET), blood tests, and biopsy. A biopsy — laboratory analysis of tissue — is usually needed to confirm diagnosis and guide treatment.

**Q: What are the main treatments?**
Surgery, chemotherapy, radiotherapy, immunotherapy, targeted therapy, and hormone therapy — often in combination. The right treatment depends on cancer type, stage, and patient factors.

**Q: How does cancer spread?**
Cells from the primary tumour break away and travel via blood or lymph to form new growths (metastases) in other organs. Common destinations vary by cancer type.

**Q: What is palliative care?**
Specialised support focused on symptom relief and quality of life, available alongside curative treatment at any stage — not only at end of life.

**Q: When should I seek emergency care?**
Seek emergency care for severe bleeding, sudden breathlessness or chest pain, high fever with chills during chemotherapy (neutropenic sepsis), sudden neurological changes, or seizures.

## Cancer by the Numbers

- **10 million deaths** globally each year (WHO, 2024).
- **19.3 million new cases** worldwide annually.
- **Top cancers by incidence:** breast, lung, colorectal, prostate, and stomach.
- **~40–50%** of cancers are linked to preventable risk factors.
- **5-year survival rates** vary widely — >90% for early-stage breast and prostate cancers; <20% for late-stage lung and liver cancers.
- **1 in 5 people** worldwide will develop cancer in their lifetime.
