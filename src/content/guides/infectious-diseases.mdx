# Infectious Diseases — Guide Hub

Infectious diseases are caused by bacteria, viruses, fungi, or parasites. They can spread between people, from animals, through food and water, or from the environment. Every human life is repeatedly shaped by infection — from the childhood illnesses that our vaccines now prevent to the respiratory viruses that circulate every winter, to the emerging pathogens that remind us our relationship with the microbial world is never fully settled.

Most infections are mild and self-limiting. Some, however, are life-threatening — including bacterial meningitis, sepsis, tuberculosis, and severe pneumonia. Globally, infectious diseases remain among the leading causes of death: lower respiratory infections, tuberculosis, HIV/AIDS, and diarrhoeal diseases kill millions every year, predominantly in low-income settings and among children under five. Antibiotic-resistant infections now cause over **1.27 million deaths annually worldwide** — and the number is rising.

Vaccination, hygiene, clean water, antimicrobial stewardship, and early treatment remain the most powerful tools for prevention and control. This hub connects all the major infectious disease guides on PatientGuide.

## Common Symptoms & When to Seek Urgent Help

### Seek Emergency Care Immediately For:

- High fever with stiff neck or severe headache (possible meningitis)
- Rapidly spreading, painful red rash
- Signs of sepsis: confusion, very high or low temperature, rapid breathing, mottled skin
- Difficulty breathing not explained by a known condition
- Loss of consciousness or seizures
- Suspected anaphylaxis after vaccination or medication

## Measles in Focus

Measles re-emerges when vaccination rates fall below ~95% — the level needed for herd immunity. It is not a mild illness. Measles can cause encephalitis, blindness, and death. It also causes immune amnesia — wiping out immune memory built up over years — leaving survivors more vulnerable to other infections for months or years after recovery.

Current outbreaks in the United States and globally reflect falling vaccination confidence more than any change in the virus itself.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What is an infectious disease?**
A disease caused by microorganisms — bacteria, viruses, fungi, or parasites — that can spread between people or from the environment. Most infections are mild and self-limiting; some are life-threatening. The distinction between bacterial, viral, and fungal infections matters clinically because it determines what treatment works.

**How do infections spread?**
Through respiratory droplets or aerosols (influenza, measles, COVID-19), direct contact (cellulitis, STIs), contaminated food or water (gastroenteritis, typhoid), blood and body fluids (hepatitis B, HIV), insect bites (Lyme disease, West Nile virus), or vertical transmission from mother to child (hepatitis B, rubella).

**What is antibiotic resistance and why does it matter?**
Antibiotic resistance occurs when bacteria evolve to survive antibiotic treatment. Resistant infections already cause over 1.27 million deaths annually worldwide and are projected to become one of the leading causes of death by 2050 if trends continue. Key drivers include overuse in humans and agriculture. See [Antibiotic Resistance](/guides/antibiotic-resistance).

**When should I seek emergency care for an infection?**
Seek emergency care immediately for high fever with stiff neck or severe headache, rapidly spreading painful rash, signs of sepsis (confusion, rapid breathing, mottled skin), difficulty breathing, or loss of consciousness. These are potential medical emergencies. See [Bacterial Meningitis](/guides/bacterial-meningitis) and [Sepsis](/guides/sepsis).

**How do I protect myself when travelling to high-risk areas?**
Verify routine vaccinations are current; get destination-specific vaccines at least 4–6 weeks before travel; take antimalarials where appropriate; use insect repellent in vector-risk areas; practise food and water safety. See [Measles — Travel](/guides/measles-travel) for measles-specific guidance.

**What is the difference between viral and bacterial infections — and does it affect treatment?**
Bacterial infections can often be treated with antibiotics. Viral infections cannot — antivirals exist for some (influenza, HIV, hepatitis B), but many require supportive care only. Taking antibiotics for a viral infection does not help and contributes to resistance. A clinician can determine which type of infection you have through examination and, where needed, testing.
