faq:
  - q: "How often will I be monitored after bowel cancer treatment?"
    a: "Surveillance protocols vary by centre and stage, but typically include clinical review and CEA blood tests every 3–6 months for the first 2–3 years, with colonoscopy at 1 year post-surgery and periodically thereafter. Imaging (CT scan) is usually arranged for higher-risk stages. Your oncology team will outline your individual follow-up plan."
  - q: "What symptoms might suggest a bowel cancer recurrence?"
    a: "Report any of the following to your doctor promptly: rectal bleeding, persistent change in bowel habit, unexplained weight loss, new abdominal or pelvic pain, or unexplained fatigue. While these can have benign causes, recurrence needs to be excluded."
  - q: "Will I need a stoma permanently after bowel cancer surgery?"
    a: "Not always. Some people require a temporary stoma (colostomy or ileostomy) to protect a bowel join while it heals, which can later be reversed. Others may need a permanent stoma, particularly for low rectal cancers. Your surgeon will discuss what is expected for your specific operation."
  - q: "How does bowel cancer treatment affect bowel function long term?"
    a: "Many people experience changes to bowel function after surgery and/or radiotherapy — including increased frequency, urgency, loose stools, or difficulty controlling wind or stool. These effects vary with treatment type and improve over time for many people. Bowel rehabilitation services and dietary guidance can help."
  - q: "Can lifestyle changes reduce the risk of bowel cancer coming back?"
    a: "Evidence supports maintaining a healthy weight, being physically active, not smoking, and limiting alcohol and processed meat as factors associated with lower recurrence risk and better overall survival. These recommendations are consistent with general cancer survivorship guidance."

## Intro

Completing treatment for bowel cancer is a major milestone. Survivorship focuses on monitoring for recurrence, managing side effects, and supporting long-term physical and emotional recovery.

---

## Key Points

- Regular follow-up reduces risk of missed recurrence.
- Side effects can persist after treatment.
- Lifestyle changes support long-term health.
- Psychological recovery is important.

---

## Follow-Up Care

Monitoring may include:

- Regular clinical review
- Blood tests (including CEA)
- Colonoscopy
- Imaging in some cases

Follow-up schedules vary by stage.

---

## Long-Term Effects

Possible long-term effects include:

- Bowel habit changes
- Fatigue
- Neuropathy (after chemotherapy)
- Sexual or urinary changes

Support is available — these issues are common.

---

## Recurrence Risk

Risk depends on initial stage and tumor characteristics.

Regular follow-up is essential during the first 3–5 years after treatment.

---

## Emotional Recovery

Anxiety about recurrence is common.

Support groups, counseling, and structured survivorship plans can help.
