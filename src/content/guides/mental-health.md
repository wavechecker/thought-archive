# Mental Health — Guide Hub

Mental health conditions affect more than **one billion people worldwide** — roughly one in eight people alive today. They are a leading cause of disability globally, accounting for 1 in 5 years lived with disability, yet stigma, limited resources, and access barriers mean the majority of those affected go untreated. The hopeful reality is that effective treatments exist — from therapy and medication to lifestyle and community support. Recovery is possible, and **early intervention saves lives**.

This hub is the central reference point for mental health on PatientGuide. It covers the full spectrum: from recognising a crisis and getting urgent help, to understanding the conditions that affect most people (depression and anxiety), to the **everyday lifestyle strategies that build resilience and protect long-term psychological health**. Whether you are looking for yourself, for someone you care about, or simply to understand the landscape, start here.

## How This Cluster Fits Together

Mental health on PatientGuide is organised across three layers:

**1. Crisis & Clinical Care** — What to do in an emergency, how to recognise serious conditions like depression and anxiety, and when and how to seek professional help. These guides prioritise safety first.

**2. Conditions** — Evidence-based explanations of the most common mental health conditions: what they are, how they are diagnosed, and what treatments work. Currently covers [Depression](/guides/depression), [Anxiety](/guides/anxiety), and the global burden of mental illness via [Global Mental Health](/guides/global-mental-health).

**3. The Mental Health Toolkit** — The lifestyle and behavioural layer. These are strategies anyone can use — as standalone habits or alongside clinical care. The [Mental Health Toolkit hub](/guides/mental-health-toolkit) acts as a doorway into seven individual guides covering sleep, exercise, nutrition, light, social connection, mindfulness, and reducing harmful inputs.

The guides in each layer link to one another. A person reading about depression will find pathways into both clinical treatment guides and lifestyle guides. A person exploring the toolkit will find links back to crisis support. The cluster is designed so that you cannot get lost — regardless of where you enter, you can find what you need.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What is the difference between mental health and mental illness?**
Mental health refers to your overall psychological well-being — how you think, feel, and cope with daily life. Mental illness refers to diagnosed conditions such as depression, anxiety disorders, or schizophrenia that significantly impair functioning. You can have good mental health without ever having a mental illness, and you can live well with a mental illness with the right support.

**When should I see a doctor about my mental health?**
See a doctor if your mood, thoughts, or behaviour are persistently affecting your work, relationships, or daily functioning; if you are using alcohol or substances to cope; if you are having thoughts of self-harm or suicide; or if symptoms have lasted more than two weeks. Early help leads to better outcomes.

**Can lifestyle changes really improve depression or anxiety?**
Yes. Exercise, sleep, diet, social connection, and stress management all have meaningful, evidence-backed effects on mood. Regular aerobic exercise can reduce depression symptoms with effects comparable to antidepressant medication in some populations. Lifestyle strategies are not replacements for professional treatment in moderate-to-severe illness, but they are powerful adjuncts.

**Are antidepressants addictive?**
Antidepressants are not addictive in the same way as alcohol or opioids — they do not cause cravings or compulsive drug-seeking. However, some antidepressants, particularly SSRIs and SNRIs, can cause discontinuation symptoms if stopped suddenly. Always taper under medical supervision.

**How do I support someone who is struggling with their mental health?**
Listen without judgement. Avoid minimising what they are going through. Encourage professional help without pressuring. Offer practical support — accompanying them to appointments, checking in regularly. If you are worried about immediate safety, stay with the person and contact crisis services. Our [Mental Health First Aid guide](/guides/mental-health-first-aid) explains the core steps.

**What should I do in a mental health emergency?**
If someone is in immediate danger of harming themselves or others, call emergency services (000 in Australia, 999 in the UK, 911 in the US). If there is no immediate danger but the person is in crisis, contact a crisis line. See the **Get Help Now** section below. Do not leave the person alone until help arrives.

## Get Help Now

If you or someone you know is in immediate crisis, help is available:

- **United States:** Call or text **988** (Suicide & Crisis Lifeline)
- **United Kingdom:** Call **116 123** (Samaritans)
- **Australia:** Call **13 11 14** (Lifeline)
- **Canada:** Call or text **988** (Suicide Crisis Helpline)

If outside these regions, please check local emergency numbers. **You are not alone — support is available.**
