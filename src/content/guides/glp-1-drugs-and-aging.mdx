faq:
  - q: "What are GLP-1 drugs?"
    a: "GLP-1 receptor agonists are medications that improve blood glucose control and reduce appetite. They are approved for type 2 diabetes and obesity management and include drugs such as semaglutide and liraglutide."
  - q: "Why are GLP-1 drugs discussed in relation to aging?"
    a: "GLP-1 drugs reduce major cardiometabolic risk factors — including excess weight, blood pressure, and blood glucose — which are key drivers of age-related disease. They may also have anti-inflammatory effects that could benefit healthspan."
  - q: "Do GLP-1 drugs extend lifespan?"
    a: "There is no definitive human evidence that GLP-1 drugs extend lifespan. Their strongest evidence is for reducing cardiovascular events and improving metabolic outcomes, which may indirectly support longer healthspan."
  - q: "Are GLP-1 drugs appropriate for healthy adults seeking longevity benefits?"
    a: "GLP-1 drugs are approved for obesity and type 2 diabetes. Use in metabolically healthy, non-obese adults is off-label with limited evidence and uncertain long-term safety — this should be discussed with a clinician."
  - q: "What are the most important lifestyle foundations for healthy aging?"
    a: "Exercise, sleep quality, nutrition, and managing cardiometabolic risk remain the most evidence-backed foundations for healthy aging. No medication replaces these fundamentals."

## Intro

GLP-1 drugs are now central to obesity and metabolic medicine — and they’re increasingly discussed as potential “longevity” drugs. This guide will map what’s known, what’s plausible, and what’s still unknown.

## Key Points

- GLP-1 drugs have strong evidence for metabolic outcomes
- “Longevity” effects are mostly indirect and still being studied
- Any benefits likely come through reduced cardiometabolic risk

## FAQ

**Q: What are GLP-1 drugs?**  
A: Medications used for diabetes/obesity that reduce appetite and improve metabolic control.

**Q: Why might they affect aging?**  
A: They may reduce chronic metabolic stress and inflammation linked to age-related disease.

**Q: Do they extend lifespan?**  
A: Not definitively proven in humans; strongest evidence is for improved cardiometabolic outcomes.
