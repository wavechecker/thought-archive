faq:
  - q: "At what age does cervical screening start?"
    a: "Most programs begin screening at age 25 (some at 21 in the United States for Pap smear-based programs). Starting age and test type vary by country. Follow your national program's invitation schedule."
  - q: "Why is cervical screening not recommended before age 25?"
    a: "HPV infection is very common in younger people, and most infections clear without causing harm. Screening before 25 leads to high rates of false positives, unnecessary follow-up procedures, and potential over-treatment of lesions that would have resolved naturally. The cervix at this age is also more susceptible to transient HPV-related changes."
  - q: "What happens if my cervical screening result is abnormal?"
    a: "An abnormal result leads to further assessment — this may include reflex cytology (looking at cells), colposcopy (magnified examination of the cervix), or biopsy. An abnormal screening result is not a diagnosis of cancer. Most abnormalities are either low-grade or caused by HPV that will clear without treatment."
  - q: "Do I still need screening if I have had the HPV vaccine?"
    a: "Yes. Current guidelines recommend continuing cervical screening regardless of vaccination status. The vaccine reduces risk substantially but does not eliminate it entirely. Screening intervals and test types may evolve as vaccinated cohorts are followed over time."
  - q: "When does cervical screening stop?"
    a: "Most programs stop routine screening around age 65–70, provided recent screens have been negative. People who have not been regularly screened or who have had recent abnormal results may need to continue beyond this age. Follow-up for past abnormalities is managed separately from routine screening."

## Key Points

- Screening recommendations change with age
- Younger women clear HPV more easily
- Risk persists into later life
- Stopping too early increases cancer risk

---

## Typical Screening Timeline

### Under 25
- Often no screening or Pap smear only
- HPV infections are common and transient

### Ages 25–29
- HPV test every 5 years **or**
- Pap smear every 3 years

### Ages 30–49
- Primary HPV testing every 5 years
- Co-testing in some regions

### Ages 50–64
- Continued HPV screening
- Risk remains despite menopause

### Over 65
- Screening may stop **only if** prior tests were consistently normal
