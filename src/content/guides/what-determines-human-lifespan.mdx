  - q: "Is human lifespan mostly determined by genetics?"
    a: "No. Genetics contributes, but environment, disease exposure, socioeconomic conditions, and modifiable risk factors play a larger role at the population level. Estimates suggest genetics accounts for around 25–35% of lifespan variation."
  - q: "Why did average human lifespan increase so dramatically in the 20th century?"
    a: "The largest gains came from reducing extrinsic mortality — clean water and sanitation, vaccines, antibiotics, safer food systems, and improved emergency and cardiovascular care. These advances reduced deaths from infection, trauma, and childbirth."
  - q: "What are the biggest modifiable determinants of lifespan today?"
    a: "Cardiometabolic risk factors are the most influential — blood pressure, smoking, blood glucose, obesity, and blood lipids. Managing these reduces the leading causes of premature death in high-income countries."
  - q: "What is the difference between lifespan and healthspan?"
    a: "Lifespan is the total number of years a person lives. Healthspan is the number of those years spent in good health and functional independence. Much of longevity medicine focuses on extending healthspan alongside lifespan."
  - q: "Can lifestyle changes meaningfully extend lifespan?"
    a: "Yes. Not smoking, regular physical activity, maintaining a healthy weight, managing blood pressure and cholesterol, and avoiding harmful alcohol use each have demonstrated longevity benefits — and the effects are cumulative."

## Intro

Human lifespan is shaped by two broad forces:

- **Intrinsic biology** (the aging process and inherited risk)
- **Extrinsic risk** (infection, trauma, environment, and social conditions)

Understanding lifespan means understanding how these forces interact across a lifetime.

## Key Points

- Lifespan is not “genes vs lifestyle” — it’s biology *plus* exposure and risk.
- Public health primarily extends lifespan by reducing extrinsic mortality.
- As extrinsic mortality falls, intrinsic aging becomes more visible.
- Healthspan is often the more actionable target. (See: [Healthspan vs Lifespan: What’s the Difference?](/guides/healthspan-vs-lifespan))

## The Big Determinants

### 1) Early-life conditions
Nutrition, infection burden, stress, and access to care in childhood can influence lifelong risk.

### 2) Cardiometabolic risk
Blood pressure, glucose, smoking, obesity, and lipids strongly influence survival.

### 3) Socioeconomic conditions
Income, education, housing, and access to care shape exposure to risk and chronic disease management.

### 4) Genetics
Genetics can influence risk pathways (lipids, inflammation, dementia risk, etc.). Effects often become more noticeable at older ages.

### 5) Intrinsic vs extrinsic mortality
See: [Intrinsic vs Extrinsic Mortality](/guides/intrinsic-vs-extrinsic-mortality)

## FAQ

**Q: If lifespan is partly genetic, can you “outrun” your genes?**  
A: Often you can reduce risk substantially by changing exposures and managing modifiable risk factors.
