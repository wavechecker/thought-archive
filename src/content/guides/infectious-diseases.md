  - question: "What is an infectious disease?"
    answer: "An infectious disease is caused by pathogenic microorganisms — bacteria, viruses, fungi, or parasites — that can be passed from person to person, from animals to people, or through environmental exposure. Some infections are mild and self-limiting; others are life-threatening."
  - question: "How do infectious diseases spread?"
    answer: "Transmission routes include respiratory droplets and aerosols (influenza, COVID-19, measles, tuberculosis), direct contact with infected individuals or surfaces, contaminated food or water, bloodborne transmission (hepatitis B, HIV), insect or animal bites (Lyme disease, rabies, West Nile virus), and vertical transmission from mother to child."
  - question: "How do vaccines prevent infectious diseases?"
    answer: "Vaccines train the immune system to recognise and respond to a specific pathogen without causing disease. They do this by introducing an antigen — a weakened or inactivated form of the pathogen, a protein subunit, or (in mRNA vaccines) instructions for the immune system to make one. This primes memory cells so that if the real pathogen is encountered later, the immune response is faster and stronger."
  - question: "What are the most important vaccines for adults?"
    answer: "For most adults in high-income countries: annual influenza vaccine, COVID-19 primary series and boosters as recommended, tetanus-diphtheria-pertussis (Td/Tdap) every 10 years, MMR (measles-mumps-rubella) if not previously immune, HPV vaccine (up to age 45 depending on country), hepatitis B if not vaccinated, and pneumococcal vaccine for those over 65 or with risk conditions."
  - question: "What is antibiotic resistance?"
    answer: "Antibiotic resistance occurs when bacteria evolve to survive exposure to antibiotics, making infections harder or impossible to treat. It is accelerated by overuse and misuse of antibiotics in humans and agriculture. Resistant organisms such as MRSA, drug-resistant tuberculosis, and drug-resistant gonorrhoea are a growing global health threat."
  - question: "What is the difference between a virus and a bacterium?"
    answer: "Bacteria are single-celled living organisms that can reproduce independently. They can be treated with antibiotics when they cause infection. Viruses are much smaller and require a host cell to replicate. They cannot be treated with antibiotics; antiviral drugs exist for some viral infections, but prevention through vaccination is often the most effective strategy."
  - question: "When should I seek emergency care for an infection?"
    answer: "Seek emergency care for high fever with neck stiffness, severe headache or photophobia (possible meningitis), rapidly spreading red skin with pain (possible cellulitis or necrotising infection), signs of sepsis (high fever or abnormally low temperature, rapid breathing, confusion, or mottled skin), difficulty breathing, or loss of consciousness."
  - question: "What are vaccine-preventable diseases?"
    answer: "Diseases for which effective vaccines exist include measles, mumps, rubella, polio, whooping cough (pertussis), tetanus, diphtheria, hepatitis A and B, HPV-related cancers and warts, influenza, COVID-19, typhoid, yellow fever, rabies, and meningococcal disease, among others."
  - question: "How does vaccination protect communities?"
    answer: "When enough people in a community are immune — through vaccination or prior infection — the pathogen cannot find enough susceptible hosts to spread widely. This 'herd immunity' or community protection also protects people who cannot be vaccinated, such as newborns and immunocompromised individuals."
  - question: "Is it safe to get multiple vaccines at once?"
    answer: "Yes. Combination and simultaneous vaccines are rigorously tested for safety and efficacy. The immune system can handle multiple antigens simultaneously. Combination vaccines (such as MMR or DTaP) also reduce the number of injections needed, improving adherence to vaccination schedules."

# Infectious Diseases — Guide Hub

Infectious diseases are caused by bacteria, viruses, fungi, or parasites and can spread between people, from animals, through food and water, or from the environment. They range from mild and self-limiting illnesses — a cold, a urinary tract infection — to life-threatening conditions such as bacterial meningitis, sepsis, or tuberculosis. Globally, infectious diseases remain among the leading causes of death, particularly in children and in low-income settings.

Vaccination, good hygiene, clean water, antimicrobial stewardship, and early treatment are the most effective tools we have. This hub connects guides on the most important infectious diseases and vaccine-preventable conditions. It is organised to help patients, families, and caregivers navigate specific diseases, understand how vaccines work, and know when to seek urgent help.

If you are concerned about signs of meningitis, sepsis, or a rapidly worsening infection, seek emergency care immediately.

## Common Symptoms & When to Seek Urgent Help

**Symptoms that warrant prompt (non-emergency) medical review:**
- Fever lasting more than 3–4 days without an obvious cause
- Localised skin redness, warmth, or swelling that is spreading
- Persistent cough (especially with blood or night sweats)
- Unusual rash with fever
- Symptoms of a urinary tract infection that don't improve in 24–48 hours

**Seek emergency care immediately for:**
- High fever with stiff neck, severe headache, or aversion to light — **possible bacterial meningitis**
- A rapidly spreading, painful red rash (possible septicaemia or necrotising fasciitis)
- Signs of sepsis: high or very low temperature, fast breathing, confusion, mottled or pale skin
- Difficulty breathing not explained by asthma or a known condition
- Loss of consciousness or seizures with fever
- Suspected anaphylaxis after vaccination or antibiotic use

## Measles in Focus

Measles consistently re-emerges when vaccination rates fall below the ~95% threshold needed for herd immunity. It is not a mild childhood illness — it can cause brain damage, blindness, and death, and a measles infection can suppress immune memory for other diseases for months or years. ## FAQ

**Q: What is an infectious disease?**
A disease caused by pathogenic microorganisms — bacteria, viruses, fungi, or parasites — that can spread from person to person, animal to person, or through the environment.

**Q: How do infections spread?**
Routes include respiratory droplets and aerosols, direct contact, contaminated food or water, blood and body fluids, insect or animal bites, and mother-to-child transmission.

**Q: How do vaccines prevent infections?**
Vaccines introduce a harmless antigen that trains the immune system to recognise and rapidly destroy the real pathogen if encountered later — without causing the disease itself.

**Q: What are the most important vaccines for adults?**
Annual influenza, COVID-19 (primary series and boosters), Tdap/Td booster every 10 years, MMR (if not previously immune), HPV (up to age 45 in most countries), hepatitis B (if unvaccinated), and pneumococcal vaccine for older or high-risk adults.

**Q: What is antibiotic resistance?**
When bacteria evolve to survive antibiotic treatment, making infections harder to cure. Overuse and misuse of antibiotics in humans and agriculture accelerates this process.

**Q: What is the difference between a virus and a bacterium?**
Bacteria are single-celled organisms that reproduce independently and respond to antibiotics. Viruses are much smaller, replicate only inside host cells, and do not respond to antibiotics — prevention through vaccination is often the most effective strategy.

**Q: When should I seek emergency care for an infection?**
Immediately for: stiff neck with high fever, rapidly spreading skin redness, signs of sepsis (confusion, rapid breathing, very high or low temperature), difficulty breathing, or loss of consciousness.

**Q: What are vaccine-preventable diseases?**
Diseases for which effective vaccines exist: measles, mumps, rubella, polio, whooping cough, tetanus, diphtheria, hepatitis A/B, HPV-related cancers, influenza, COVID-19, typhoid, yellow fever, meningococcal disease, and others.

**Q: How does vaccination protect communities?**
When enough people are immune, a pathogen cannot find enough susceptible hosts to sustain spread — protecting even those who cannot be vaccinated.

**Q: Is it safe to get multiple vaccines at once?**
Yes. Simultaneous administration is rigorously tested and safe. The immune system handles multiple antigens without difficulty — this is how combination vaccines work.
