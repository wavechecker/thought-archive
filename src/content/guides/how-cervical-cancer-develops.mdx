faq:
  - q: "How does cervical cancer develop?"
    a: "Cervical cancer usually develops slowly over 10–15 years, starting with HPV infection, progressing through persistent infection and pre-cancerous cell changes, to invasive cancer if undetected and untreated."
  - q: "Does every HPV infection lead to cervical cancer?"
    a: "No. Most HPV infections clear on their own. Cancer only develops in a minority of people who have a persistent high-risk HPV infection that goes undetected through the pre-cancerous stage."
  - q: "Where does cervical screening fit in this process?"
    a: "Screening is designed to detect abnormal cells at the pre-cancerous stage — before cancer has formed — when treatment is highly effective and the cancer can be prevented."
  - q: "Can cervical cancer be prevented?"
    a: "Yes. HPV vaccination significantly reduces the risk from the most cancer-causing HPV types, and regular cervical screening can detect and treat pre-cancerous changes before they develop into cancer."

    a: "Early-stage cervical cancer is often asymptomatic, which is why regular screening matters. Symptoms in more advanced disease can include abnormal vaginal bleeding, unusual discharge, or pelvic pain — report these to a doctor."

## The Long Timeline

Cervical cancer usually develops slowly, over **10–15 years**.

---

### Step 1: HPV Exposure
HPV infection is common and often clears naturally.

### Step 2: Persistent Infection
In some cases, the virus remains for years.

### Step 3: Precancerous Changes
Abnormal cells form — still reversible.

### Step 4: Localised Cancer
Cancer develops within the cervix.

### Step 5: Invasive Disease
Cancer spreads beyond the cervix if untreated.

---

## Where Screening Works

Screening interrupts this process at **Steps 2–3**, long before cancer forms.
