## Intro

Abdominal pain is one of the most common â€” and most anxiety-provoking â€” symptoms in children.

Most cases are benign (constipation, viral illness, functional pain). A smaller number are **time-sensitive** (appendicitis, torsion, obstruction). The difference is usually **pattern, progression, and behaviour**, not how dramatic the pain sounds.

---

## Key Principles (How clinicians think about this)

- **Progression beats severity:** pain that steadily worsens is more concerning than pain that sounds severe but improves.
- **Movement matters:** pain with walking, jumping, or coughing suggests peritoneal irritation.
- **Localisation matters:** pain that becomes focused in one spot is more concerning than diffuse pain.
- **Behaviour is a vital sign:** a very still, withdrawn child is more concerning than a distractible one.
- **Constipation is common and underestimated**, even when a child still passes stool.

## Common Causes by Pattern

### Usually low-risk
- Constipation  
- Viral illness / gastroenteritis  
- Gas or indigestion  
- Functional (anxiety-related) abdominal pain  

### Needs clinician review
- Urinary tract infection  
- Strep throat (can present as belly pain)  
- Food intolerance  
- Reflux  

### Time-sensitive â€œdonâ€™t missâ€
- **Appendicitis**
- **Testicular torsion**
- Intussusception (younger children)
- Ovarian torsion (adolescents)
- Diabetic ketoacidosis (thirst, frequent urination, vomiting)
- Pneumonia (referred abdominal pain)

---

## What to Expect if You Seek Care

Assessment often includes:
- Vital signs and hydration check
- Abdominal exam (sometimes repeated)
- Urine testing
- Blood tests if indicated
- Ultrasound if appendicitis or torsion suspected
- Clear safety-net instructions if discharged

*A normal early test does not always exclude appendicitis â€” reassessment matters.*
