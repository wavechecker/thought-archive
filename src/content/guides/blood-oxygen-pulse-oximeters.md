## Intro

Pulse oximeters estimate blood oxygen saturation (**SpO₂**) by shining light through your finger and measuring how much is absorbed by oxygenated haemoglobin. They are widely used in hospitals and increasingly at home. Understanding what the numbers mean — and where they can mislead — is essential for using them safely.

## Key Points

- Normal SpO₂ for healthy adults at sea level is typically **95–100%**
- Readings **≤92% at rest** or a **sudden drop** from baseline warrant medical advice
- Chronic lung disease, altitude, or heart conditions may shift an individual's "normal" range — always follow your clinician's guidance
- Nail polish, motion, cold fingers, and poor circulation can all produce **false low readings**
- A pulse oximeter measures only oxygen saturation — not breathing effort, carbon dioxide levels, or overall cardiorespiratory health

## What SpO₂ Measures

Haemoglobin in red blood cells carries oxygen around the body. SpO₂ is the percentage of haemoglobin currently saturated (carrying oxygen). A pulse oximeter estimates this using two wavelengths of light: oxygenated and deoxygenated haemoglobin absorb light differently.

The reading reflects oxygen delivery from the lungs into the blood — but not whether cells are using that oxygen effectively, nor whether breathing effort is normal. This is why SpO₂ is one piece of clinical information, not the whole picture.

## How It's Used in Practice

**At home:**
- Monitoring for people with [asthma](/guides/asthma), [COPD](/guides/copd), heart failure, or sleep apnoea
- During and after acute respiratory illness (e.g. COVID-19), as directed by a clinician
- Tracking exercise tolerance in people with known lung or heart conditions

**In clinical settings:**
- Continuous monitoring during surgery and recovery
- Titrating supplemental oxygen therapy
- Assessing respiratory deterioration in acutely unwell patients

### How to take an accurate reading

1. Warm your hands before measuring
2. Remove nail polish or use an unpolished finger
3. Sit still and rest for 5 minutes; do not talk
4. Keep the probe steady until the reading stabilises (usually 30–60 seconds)
5. Take several readings and use the average; discard outliers caused by movement

## Limitations and Misunderstandings

- **Cold or poorly perfused fingers** reduce blood flow to the fingertip and make readings unreliable; warm hands first
- **Motion** causes signal interference — always measure at rest, not during activity
- **Skin tone bias** — research has identified that some consumer and clinical oximeters are less accurate in people with darker skin tones; be aware of this limitation
- **Carbon monoxide poisoning** produces a falsely normal SpO₂ because CO-bound haemoglobin absorbs light similarly to oxygenated haemoglobin
- **Anaemia** does not lower SpO₂ directly — a person with severe anaemia can have normal saturation yet still be oxygen-deficient at the tissue level
- SpO₂ does not measure **carbon dioxide (CO₂)** retention, an important concern in COPD and other conditions

## FAQ

**What is a normal blood oxygen level (SpO₂)?**
For healthy adults at sea level, a normal SpO₂ is typically 95–100%. Values below 95% may warrant monitoring; readings at or below 92% at rest generally require medical attention.

**When should I seek emergency care for low SpO₂?**
Call emergency services if your SpO₂ is at or below 92% at rest, drops suddenly from your usual baseline, or if low readings occur alongside shortness of breath, chest pain, confusion, or blue lips.

**Does altitude affect normal SpO₂?**
Yes. SpO₂ naturally drops at high altitude due to lower atmospheric oxygen pressure. People living or travelling at altitude may have readings of 90–94% without clinical concern. Follow your clinician's guidance.

**Can nail polish affect pulse oximeter readings?**
Yes. Dark nail polish — especially blue, black, or purple — can interfere with the light sensor and produce artificially low readings. Remove polish or use a different unpolished finger.

**Why does my oximeter give different readings each time?**
Motion, cold or poorly perfused fingers, low blood pressure, and device quality all contribute to variability. Warm your hands, sit still for 5 minutes, and average several readings for greater reliability.

**Is a home pulse oximeter as accurate as a hospital device?**
Consumer-grade oximeters are less accurate than clinical-grade devices, especially at lower saturations. Use them as a general monitoring guide rather than a definitive clinical measurement.

**What SpO₂ reading is considered dangerous?**
Below 90% is generally concerning and warrants prompt medical evaluation. Readings below 85% are a medical emergency regardless of symptoms. Always interpret readings in clinical context.

**Can anxiety or hyperventilation affect SpO₂?**
Hyperventilation often slightly raises SpO₂. Anxiety itself does not cause low oxygen saturation. If you are breathless and your SpO₂ is low, a medical cause should be investigated.

**Is a single SpO₂ reading enough to act on?**
Not usually. A single home reading — especially a low one — should prompt repeat measurement with correct technique before drawing conclusions. Persistent low readings with symptoms require medical assessment.

**What should I do while waiting for emergency help if SpO₂ is low?**
Sit upright, stay calm, and avoid exertion. If you have been prescribed supplemental oxygen and it is available, use it as directed. Call emergency services for readings below 92% with symptoms.
