faq:
  - q: "Does a fracture mean I have osteoporosis?"
    a: "Not every fracture does — high-energy trauma can fracture healthy bone. However, a low-trauma fracture (from a minor fall from standing height or less) in an adult over 50 is a strong indicator of osteoporosis and should prompt bone density testing and treatment review."
  - q: "What steps should be taken after a fracture to prevent another one?"
    a: "Secondary fracture prevention includes a bone density assessment (DEXA scan), review of bone-protective medications, calcium and vitamin D optimisation, falls risk assessment, physiotherapy for strength and balance, and addressing any underlying conditions affecting bone health."
  - q: "Should I start osteoporosis medication after a fracture?"
    a: "This depends on your bone density, fracture risk score, and other factors. Many guidelines recommend starting bone-protective medication after a low-trauma fracture, particularly of the hip or spine. The decision should be made with your doctor."
  - q: "How can I reduce my risk of falling after a fracture?"
    a: "Falls prevention includes strength and balance exercises (such as tai chi or supervised physiotherapy), reviewing medications that cause dizziness, checking eyesight and hearing, making the home safer, and using walking aids if needed."
  - q: "How long does recovery from a hip fracture take?"
    a: "Recovery from a hip fracture typically takes several months, with full recovery potentially taking a year or more. Early mobilisation, physiotherapy, and adequate nutrition — including protein — are key to a good outcome."

## Intro
A hip fracture or other low-impact fracture is often the first clear sign of osteoporosis.  
While surgery and rehabilitation repair the break, it’s just as important to treat the underlying bone weakness to prevent future fractures.

## Key Points
- A fracture after a minor fall usually means bones are fragile from osteoporosis.  
- Rehab restores movement and independence, but bone treatment reduces the chance of another fracture.  
- Medications, exercise, nutrition, and fall prevention are all part of long-term management.  

## Recovery After Fracture
- **Rehabilitation:** physical therapy for strength, balance, walking confidence.  
- **Pain management:** medication, heat/ice, support devices.  
- **Mobility aids:** canes, walkers, hip protectors.  
- **Home safety:** remove hazards, install grab bars, improve lighting.  

## Tackling Osteoporosis
- **Medical assessment:** DEXA scan and lab tests.  
- **Medications:** bisphosphonates, denosumab, or anabolic agents.  
- **Nutrition:** calcium + vitamin D.  
- **Exercise:** once cleared, weight-bearing + resistance training.  

## Why It Matters
- After a hip fracture, the risk of a second fracture is high.  
- Treating osteoporosis reduces that risk and helps maintain independence.  
- Early secondary prevention is key: every fracture is a chance to intervene.  

## FAQ
**Q: Do all fractures mean osteoporosis?**  
A: Not always, but hip and spine fractures after minor falls are highly suggestive.  

**Q: When should osteoporosis treatment start?**  
A: As soon as the fracture is stabilized — rehab and bone health should go hand-in-hand.  

**Q: What if I’m already on treatment?**  
A: Your doctor may review or adjust medication for maximum protection.
