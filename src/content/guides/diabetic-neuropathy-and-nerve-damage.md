## Summary

Diabetic neuropathy is nerve damage caused by prolonged exposure to elevated blood glucose. It is one of the most common long-term complications of diabetes, affecting approximately 50% of people with diabetes at some point. It can affect peripheral nerves (particularly in the feet and legs) and the autonomic nervous system (which controls involuntary functions like digestion and heart rate).

This guide covers:
- What diabetic neuropathy is and why it develops
- The different types, including peripheral and autonomic neuropathy
- Symptoms to recognise — including pain, numbness, and less obvious presentations
- Risk factors and who is most vulnerable
- How to prevent nerve damage through glucose control
- Foot care to prevent serious complications
- When to seek medical help

---

## What Is Diabetic Neuropathy?

Neuropathy means nerve damage or dysfunction. In people with diabetes, this arises from the effects of chronically elevated blood glucose on the small blood vessels that supply nerves and on the nerve cells themselves. Over time, this leads to impaired nerve conduction — nerves either become overactive (producing pain and abnormal sensations) or underactive (producing numbness and loss of sensation).

Peripheral nerves — the nerves that extend from the spinal cord to the limbs, skin, and organs — are most affected because they are long and have high metabolic demands. The longest nerves (supplying the feet and lower legs) are typically affected first, which is why foot symptoms are the most common presentation.

Neuropathy develops gradually. There is no single threshold of blood glucose at which it suddenly appears — it reflects cumulative damage over months and years. This makes consistent glucose management a long-term priority, not just a short-term goal.

## Types of Diabetic Neuropathy

### Peripheral Neuropathy

Peripheral neuropathy is the most common form, affecting the nerves supplying the feet, lower legs, and sometimes the hands. It follows a **"stocking and glove" pattern** — beginning at the extremities and potentially spreading upward.

**Symptoms include:**
- Numbness or reduced sensation in the feet and toes
- Tingling or "pins and needles" — often persistent, unlike the transient tingling from a compressed limb
- Burning pain, particularly at night, sometimes severe enough to disrupt sleep
- Sharp, stabbing, or electric shock-like sensations
- Sensitivity to touch — even the weight of bedsheets can cause discomfort (allodynia)
- Weakness in the feet or legs in more advanced cases

**The silent risk:** Peripheral neuropathy often causes **reduced pain and temperature sensation** alongside or even before painful symptoms. This means cuts, blisters, burns, and pressure injuries may not be felt. An unnoticed wound that goes untreated can become infected, fail to heal (due to impaired circulation), and in severe cases lead to ulceration or, in the most serious situations, amputation. This is why **foot care is inseparable from neuropathy management**.

### Autonomic Neuropathy

The autonomic nervous system regulates involuntary body functions: heart rate, blood pressure, digestion, bladder function, sweating, and sexual function. Autonomic neuropathy is less commonly recognised but clinically significant.

**Symptoms depending on the systems affected:**

**Cardiovascular autonomic neuropathy:**
- Dizziness or faintness when standing up (postural hypotension) — caused by impaired blood pressure regulation
- Resting tachycardia (persistently elevated heart rate at rest)
- Reduced ability to detect cardiac ischaemia (silent myocardial ischaemia)

**Gastrointestinal autonomic neuropathy:**
- Gastroparesis — delayed gastric emptying, causing nausea, vomiting, early fullness, and unpredictable glucose absorption after meals
- Diarrhoea, particularly at night, or alternating constipation and diarrhoea

**Bladder dysfunction:**
- Incomplete bladder emptying, urinary retention, or loss of bladder sensation
- Increased susceptibility to urinary tract infections

**Sudomotor dysfunction (sweating):**
- Abnormal sweating patterns — excessive sweating in the upper body, reduced or absent sweating in the legs and feet (contributing to dry, cracked skin)

**Sexual dysfunction:**
- Erectile dysfunction in men (neuropathy is one of several contributors alongside vascular disease)
- Reduced genital sensation or lubrication in women

### Focal and Multifocal Neuropathies

Less common forms include:
- **Mononeuropathy** — sudden involvement of a single nerve, such as the median nerve (carpel tunnel), or nerves supplying eye movement (causing double vision or drooping eyelid). Often resolves over weeks to months.
- **Diabetic amyotrophy (lumbosacral radiculoplexus neuropathy)** — severe pain and weakness in the thigh and hip, usually with weight loss; more common in older people with Type 2 diabetes.

## Risk Factors

Not everyone with diabetes develops neuropathy at the same rate. Factors that increase risk include:

- **Poorly controlled blood glucose over time** — the most significant modifiable driver; reflected in elevated HbA1c
- **Long duration of diabetes** — the longer the exposure, the higher the cumulative risk
- **High blood pressure** — damages the small vessels supplying nerves
- **Dyslipidaemia** — abnormal cholesterol or triglyceride levels contribute to vascular and nerve damage
- **Smoking** — impairs circulation, multiplying vascular and nerve risk
- **Obesity** — associated with insulin resistance and additional metabolic stress
- **Kidney disease** — diabetic nephropathy and neuropathy frequently coexist and share common risk drivers

## Prevention: The Role of Glucose Control

The evidence that consistent blood glucose management reduces neuropathy risk is strong. The Diabetes Control and Complications Trial (DCCT), a landmark clinical study in Type 1 diabetes, demonstrated that intensive glucose management significantly reduced the development and progression of neuropathy compared to conventional treatment. Its follow-up study (EDIC) showed these benefits persisted for decades.

**What this means in practice:**

- **HbA1c targets** — lowering average glucose (reflected in HbA1c) over time reduces the cumulative nerve damage that leads to neuropathy. The specific target is individual and agreed with your diabetes team, but lower average glucose consistently reduces risk.
- **Reducing time in high glucose** — not just average glucose, but the frequency and duration of high-glucose periods, contributes to nerve damage.
- **Continuous glucose monitoring (CGM)** — helps identify patterns of high glucose that finger-prick testing alone might miss, enabling more responsive adjustments.
- **Blood pressure management** — controlling hypertension reduces damage to the small blood vessels that supply nerves, independently of glucose control.
- **Not smoking** — smoking significantly worsens both vascular and nerve outcomes.
- **Cholesterol management** — elevated triglycerides in particular are associated with neuropathy risk.

Prevention is substantially more effective than treatment of established neuropathy. Waiting until symptoms develop to prioritise glucose control means nerve damage has already accumulated.

## Foot Care

For people with peripheral neuropathy, foot care is a clinical priority, not a cosmetic one. Reduced sensation means that small injuries — a blister from tight shoes, a cut from walking barefoot, or a pressure sore — can occur without being felt. Impaired circulation (which often accompanies neuropathy in diabetes) reduces healing and increases infection risk.

### Daily Foot Inspection

Inspect both feet every day — including between toes and on the sole. Look for:
- Cuts, cracks, or broken skin
- Blisters or calluses
- Redness, swelling, or warmth
- Changes to nail appearance or colour
- Any area that does not look or feel normal

A mirror can help inspect the soles. If vision is poor, ask a family member or carer to help. Any wound that is not healing, or any new swelling or redness, should be reviewed by a clinician promptly — do not wait for a scheduled appointment.

### Footwear and Skin Care

- Wear well-fitting shoes that do not rub or create pressure points. Avoid tight footwear, high heels, or shoes that compress the toes.
- Never walk barefoot, particularly on hot surfaces (beaches, pavements in summer) — burns are a significant risk when sensation is reduced.
- Dry feet thoroughly after washing, particularly between toes, to prevent fungal infections.
- Moisturise dry skin but avoid applying cream between toes (excess moisture there promotes fungal growth).
- Cut nails straight across, not into the corners. If nails are difficult to manage, a podiatrist can assist.
- Avoid using sharp instruments, corn plasters, or chemical treatments on feet without podiatry guidance.

### Clinical Foot Reviews

People with diabetes should receive an annual foot examination as part of their diabetes review. This includes:
- Assessment of sensation (monofilament testing, vibration sense)
- Vascular assessment (pulses, Doppler studies if indicated)
- Skin and nail inspection
- Review of footwear

Higher-risk feet (neuropathy, previous ulceration, impaired circulation) require more frequent review and, in some health systems, specialist podiatry input. Know your foot risk category and act accordingly.

## Managing Neuropathy Symptoms

Established neuropathy cannot generally be fully reversed. Management focuses on:

### Pain Management
Painful diabetic neuropathy is a recognised condition requiring active treatment. Effective options include:
- **Antidepressants** — duloxetine (an SNRI) and amitriptyline are commonly used and have good evidence for neuropathic pain; they act on pain pathways independent of their antidepressant effect
- **Anticonvulsants** — pregabalin and gabapentin reduce nerve excitability and are widely used for neuropathic pain
- **Topical treatments** — capsaicin cream or lidocaine patches can reduce localised pain with minimal systemic effects
- **Simple analgesia** — paracetamol may help mild symptoms; NSAIDs are generally avoided in people with diabetic nephropathy
- **Opioids** — not recommended as first-line for diabetic neuropathic pain; risks generally outweigh benefits

Pain management in neuropathy often requires trying more than one approach and combining treatments. Discuss options with your doctor or diabetes team.

### Glucose Optimisation
Even in established neuropathy, improving glucose control slows further progression and may allow modest symptomatic improvement over months. This remains the cornerstone of management alongside symptom treatment.

### Symptom Management for Autonomic Neuropathy
Gastroparesis, postural hypotension, and bladder dysfunction each have specific management strategies that should be discussed with your healthcare team. These are specialist-managed conditions.

## When to Seek Medical Help

**See your doctor or diabetes team if you develop:**
- Numbness, tingling, burning pain, or weakness in your feet or hands
- Dizziness or faintness when standing
- Unexplained nausea, vomiting, or early fullness after meals
- Bladder problems including incomplete emptying or recurrent urinary infections
- Any foot wound, ulcer, or injury that is not healing normally
- Changes to foot colour, temperature, or swelling

**Seek urgent or same-day review for:**
- A foot wound with signs of infection — increasing redness, warmth, swelling, discharge, or fever
- Rapidly spreading redness or skin changes on the foot or lower leg

Diabetic foot infections can progress quickly and require prompt clinical assessment.

## FAQ

### What does diabetic neuropathy feel like?

Peripheral neuropathy typically causes numbness, tingling, or "pins and needles" starting in the feet, often with burning pain at night. Some people develop reduced sensation — meaning injuries can occur unnoticed. Autonomic neuropathy can cause dizziness on standing, digestive symptoms, or bladder changes.

### Can diabetic neuropathy be reversed?

Established nerve damage generally cannot be fully reversed. Improving glucose control can slow further progression and may allow some recovery in early cases. Management focuses on symptom relief, foot protection, and slowing progression. Prevention through consistent glucose control before significant damage develops is substantially more effective.

### Do I have to have had diabetes for a long time to develop neuropathy?

Neuropathy is more common with longer disease duration, but it can develop earlier with poorly managed glucose. Some people have detectable nerve changes at or shortly after diagnosis. Consistent glucose management matters from the beginning.

### How often should I check my feet?

Daily. Look for cuts, blisters, redness, swelling, or any changes to the skin or nails. Neuropathy reduces sensation, so injuries may not cause pain. Any non-healing wound or unexplained change should be reviewed promptly — do not wait for scheduled appointments.

### Is diabetic neuropathy only a problem with Type 1 diabetes?

No. It affects people with both Type 1 and Type 2 diabetes. The key driver is cumulative exposure to elevated blood glucose over time. People with poorly controlled Type 2 diabetes, long duration of disease, high HbA1c, high blood pressure, or who smoke are at significant risk.

### When should I see a doctor about neuropathy symptoms?

See your doctor or diabetes team if you develop numbness, tingling, burning pain, or weakness in the feet or hands; if any foot wound is not healing normally; or if you experience dizziness on standing, unexplained digestive problems, or bladder changes. Seek urgent review for any foot wound showing signs of infection.
